c.1600 (transitive), from French submerger (14c.) or directly from Latin submergere to plunge under,
sink, overwhelm, from sub under (see <a href=/index.php?term=sub-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>sub-</a>) + mergere to plunge, immerse (see <a
href=/index.php?term=merge&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>merge</a>). Intransitive
meaning sink under water, sink out of sight is from 1650s, made common 20c. in connection with
submarines. Related: Submerged; submerging.
