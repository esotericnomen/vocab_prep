early 15c., holding of a tenement, from Anglo-French and Old French tenure a tenure, estate in land
(13c.), from Old French tenir to hold, from Vulgar Latin *tenire, from Latin tenere to hold (see <a
href=/index.php?term=tenet&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>tenet</a>). The sense of
condition or fact of holding a status, position, or occupation is first attested 1590s. Meaning
guaranteed tenure of office (usually at a university or school) is recorded from 1957. Related:
Tenured (1961).
