avarice (n.)

c.1300, from Old French avarice greed, covetousness (12c.), from Latin avaritia greed, from avarus
greedy, adjectival form of avere crave, long for.


avaricious (adj.)

late 15c., from Old French avaricios greedy, covetous (Modern French avaricieux), from avarice (see
avarice). An Old English word for it was feoh-georn. Related: Avariciously; avariciousness.


tightwad (n.)

parsimonious person, 1900, from tight in the figurative sense of close-fisted (1805) + wad (n.). The
notions of stringency and avarice also combine in Modern Greek sphiktos greedy, literally tight.


largesse (n.)

also largess, willingness to give or spend freely; munificence, c.1200, from Old French largesse a
bounty, munificence, from Vulgar Latin *largitia abundance, from Latin largus abundant (see large).
In medieval theology, the virtue whose opposite is avarice, and whose excess is prodigality [Middle
English Dictionary]. The Old French suffix -esse is from Latin -itia, added to adjectives to form
nouns of quality (compare duress, riches).
