Old English ofer beyond, above, upon, in, across, past; on high, from Proto-Germanic *uberi
(cognates: Old Saxon obar, Old Frisian over, Old Norse yfir, Old High German ubar, German &uuml;ber,
Gothic ufar over, above), from PIE *uper (see <a href=/index.php?term=super-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>super-</a>). As an adjective from Old English uffera. As an adverb from late
Old English. Sense of finished is attested from late 14c. Meaning recovered from is from 1929. In
radio communication, used to indicate the speaker has finished speaking (1926). Adjective phrase
over-the-counter is attested from 1875, originally of stocks and shares.
