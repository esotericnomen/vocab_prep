1540s, epic poem, from Middle French rhapsodie, from Latin rhapsodia, from Greek rhapsoidia verse
composition, recitation of epic poetry; a book, a lay, a canto, from rhapsodos reciter of epic
poems, literally one who stitches or strings songs together, from rhaptein to stitch, sew, weave
(see <a href=/index.php?term=wrap&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>wrap</a> (v.)) + oide
song (see <a href=/index.php?term=ode&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>ode</a>). Meaning
exalted enthusiastic feeling or expression is from 1630s. Meaning sprightly musical composition is
first recorded 1850s.
