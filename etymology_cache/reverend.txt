early 15c., worthy of respect, from Middle French reverend, from Latin reverendus (he who is) to be
respected, gerundive of revereri (see <a href=/index.php?term=reverence&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>reverence</a>). As a form of address for clergymen, it is attested from late
15c.; earlier reverent (late 14c. in this sense). Abbreviation Rev. is attested from 1721, earlier
Revd. (1690s). Very Reverend is used of deans, Right Reverend of bishops, Most Reverend of
archbishops.
