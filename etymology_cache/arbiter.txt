late 14c., from Old French arbitre or directly from Latin arbiter one who goes somewhere (as witness
or judge), in classical Latin used of spectators and eye-witnesses, in law, he who hears and decides
a case, a judge, umpire, mediator; from ad- to (see <a
href=/index.php?term=ad-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>ad-</a>) + baetere to come, go.
The specific sense of one chosen by two disputing parties to decide the matter is from 1540s. The
earliest form of the word attested in English is the fem. noun arbitress (mid-14c.) a woman who
settles disputes.
