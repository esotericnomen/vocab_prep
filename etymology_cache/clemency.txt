1550s, mildness or gentleness shown in exercise of authority, from Latin clementia calmness,
gentleness, from clemens calm, mild, related to clinare to lean (see <a
href=/index.php?term=lean&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>lean</a> (v.)) + participial
suffix -menos (also in <a href=/index.php?term=alumnus&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>alumnus</a>). For sense evolution, compare inclined in secondary meaning
disposed favorably. Earlier in same sense was clemence (late 15c.).  <br /><br />  Meaning mildness
of weather or climate is 1660s (a sense also in Latin); <a
href=/index.php?term=clement&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>clement</a> (adj.) is older
in both senses, late 15c. and 1620s respectively, but now is used only in negation and only of the
weather.
