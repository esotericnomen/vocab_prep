turncoat (n.)

1550s, from turn (v.) + coat (n.). The image is of one who attempts to hide the badge of his party
or leader. The expression to turn one's coat change principles or party is recorded from 1560s.


pliant (adj.)

late 14c., from Old French ploiant bending, supple; compliant, fickle, as a noun, turncoat (13c.),
present participle of ploier to bend (see ply (n.)). Figurative sense of easily influenced is from
c.1400. Related: Pliancy.


coat of arms (n.)

mid-14c., originally a tunic embroidered with heraldic arms (worn over armor, etc); see from coat
(n.) + arm (n.2) and compare Old French cote a armer. Sense transferred to the heraldic arms
themselves by 1560s. Hence turncoat, one who put his coat on inside-out to hide the badge of his
loyalty.


renegade (n.)

1580s, apostate, probably (with change of suffix) from Spanish renegado, originally Christian turned
Muslim, from Medieval Latin renegatus, noun use of past participle of renegare deny (see renege).
General sense of turncoat is from 1660s. The form renegate, directly from Medieval Latin, is
attested in English from late 14c. As an adjective from 1705.
