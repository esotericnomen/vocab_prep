1530s, alteration of Middle English wydderen dry up, shrivel (late 14c.), intransitive, apparently a
differentiated and special use of wederen to expose to weather (see <a
href=/index.php?term=weather&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>weather</a> (v.)). Compare
German verwittern to become weather-beaten, from Witter weather. Transitive sense from 1550s.
Related: Withered; withering; witheringly.
