1817, literally observer of beautiful forms, coined by its inventor, Scottish scientist David
Brewster (1781-1868), from Greek kalos beautiful (see <a
href=/index.php?term=Callisto&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>Callisto</a>) + eidos
shape (see <a href=/index.php?term=-oid&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>-oid</a>) + <a
href=/index.php?term=-scope&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>-scope</a>, on model of <a
href=/index.php?term=telescope&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>telescope</a>, etc. They
sold by the thousands in the few years after their invention, but Brewster failed to secure a
patent.  <br /><br />  Figurative meaning constantly changing pattern is first attested 1819 in Lord
Byron, whose publisher had sent him one of the toys. As a verb, from 1891. A kaleidophone (1827) was
invented by English physicist Sir Charles Wheatstone (1802-1875) to make sound waves visible.
