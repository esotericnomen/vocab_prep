c.1600, people or things, mingled confusedly, grouped together without order, consisting of a
disorderly mix; indiscriminate, from Latin promiscuus mixed, indiscriminate, in common, without
distinction, from pro- forward (see <a href=/index.php?term=pro-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>pro-</a>) + miscere to mix (see <a
href=/index.php?term=mix&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>mix</a> (v.)). Meaning
indiscriminate in sexual relations recorded by 1857, from <a
href=/index.php?term=promiscuity&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>promiscuity</a>. The
Latin adjective was used with conubia (such as between patricians and plebeians). Related:
Promiscuously.
