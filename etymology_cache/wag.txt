early 13c. (intransitive), waver, vacillate, lack steadfastness, probably from a Scandinavian source
(compare Old Norse vagga a cradle, Danish vugge rock a cradle, Old Swedish wagga fluctuate, rock a
cradle), and in part from Old English wagian move backwards and forwards; all from Proto-Germanic
*wag- (cognates: Old High German weggen, Gothic wagjan to wag), probably from PIE root *wegh- to
move about (see <a href=/index.php?term=weigh&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>weigh</a>).  <br /><br />  Transitive meaning move (something) back and forth
or up and down is from c.1300; of dogs and their tails from mid-15c.: and whanne they [hounds] see
the hure maystre they wol make him cheere and wagge hur tayles upon him. [Edward, Duke of York, The
Master of Game, 1456]. Related: Wagged; wagging. Wag-at-the-wall (1825) was an old name for a
hanging clock with pendulum and weights exposed.
