late 14c., not ordered, lacking order or regularity, from Latin inordinatus unordered, not arranged,
from in- not, opposite of (see <a href=/index.php?term=in-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>in-</a> (1)) + ordinatus, past participle of ordinare to set in order (see <a
href=/index.php?term=order&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>order</a>). Sense of
immoderate, excessive is from notion of not kept within orderly limits. Related: Inordinately;
inordinateness.
