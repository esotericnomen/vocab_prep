1660s, living in flocks (of animals), from Latin gregarius pertaining to a flock; of the herd, of
the common sort, common, from grex (genitive gregis) flock, herd, reduplication of PIE root *ger- to
gather together, assemble (cognates: Greek ageirein to assemble, agora assembly; Old Church Slavonic
grusti handful; Lithuanian gurgulys chaos, confusion, gurguole crowd, mass). Sense of sociable first
recorded 1789. Related: Gregariously;  gregariousness.
