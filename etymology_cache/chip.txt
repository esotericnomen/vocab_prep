early 15c., to chip (intransitive, of stone); from Old English forcippian to pare away by cutting,
cut off, verbal form of cipp small piece of wood (see <a
href=/index.php?term=chip&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>chip</a> (n.)). Transitive
meaning to cut up, cut or trim is from late 15c. Sense of break off fragments is 18c. To chip in
contribute (1861) is  American English, perhaps from card-playing. Related: Chipped; chipping.
Chipped beef attested from 1826.
