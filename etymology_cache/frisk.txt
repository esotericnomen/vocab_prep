1510s, to dance, frolic, from Middle English frisk lively (mid-15c.), from Middle French frisque
lively, brisk, from Old French frisque fresh, new; merry, animated (13c.), possibly from a Germanic
source (compare Middle Dutch vrisch fresh, Old High German frisc lively; see <a
href=/index.php?term=fresh&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>fresh</a> (adj.1)). Sense of
pat down in a search first recorded 1781. Related: Frisked; frisking. As a noun from 1520s.
