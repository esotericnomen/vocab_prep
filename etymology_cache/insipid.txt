1610s, without taste or perceptible flavor, from French insipide (16c.), from Late Latin inspidus
tasteless, from Latin in- not (see <a href=/index.php?term=in-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>in-</a> (1)) + sapidus tasty, from sapere have a taste (also be wise; see <a
href=/index.php?term=sapient&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>sapient</a>). Figurative
meaning uninteresting, dull first recorded 1640s, but it was also a secondary sense in Medieval
Latin.<blockquote>In ye coach ... went Mrs. Barlow, the King's mistress and mother to ye Duke of
Monmouth, a browne, beautifull, bold, but insipid creature. [John Evelyn, diary, Aug. 18,
1649]</blockquote>  Related: Insipidly.
