variant of <a href=/index.php?term=burst&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>burst</a> (n.),
1764, American English. For loss of -r-, compare <a href=/index.php?term=ass&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>ass</a> (n.2). Originally frolic, spree; sense of sudden failure is from 1842.
Meaning police raid or arrest is from 1938. Phrase ______ or bust as an emphatic expression attested
by 1851 in British depictions of Western U.S. dialect. Probably from earlier expression bust (one's)
boiler, by late 1840s, a reference to steamboat boilers exploding when driven too hard.
