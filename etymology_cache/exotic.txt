1590s, belonging to another country, from Middle French exotique (16c.) and directly from Latin
exoticus, from Greek exotikos foreign, literally from the outside, from exo outside (see <a
href=/index.php?term=exo-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>exo-</a>). Sense of unusual,
strange first recorded in English 1620s, from notion of alien, outlandish. In reference to strip-
teasers and dancing girls, it is first attested by 1942, American English.  <blockquote>  Exotic
dancer in the nightclub trade means a girl who goes through a few motions while wearing as few
clothes as the cops will allow in the city where she is working ... [Life, May 5, 1947]
</blockquote>  As a noun from 1640s.
