mid-14c., reuerye, wild conduct, frolic, from Old French reverie, resverie revelry, raving, delirium
(Modern French r&#234;verie), from resver to dream, wander, rave (12c., Modern French r&#234;ver),
of uncertain origin (also the root of <a href=/index.php?term=rave&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>rave</a>). Meaning daydream is first attested 1650s, a reborrowing from French.
As a type of musical composition, it is attested from 1880. Related: Reverist.
