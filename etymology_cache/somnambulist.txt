Old English sl&aelig;p sleep, sleepiness, inactivity, from Proto-Germanic *slepaz, from the root of
<a href=/index.php?term=sleep&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>sleep</a> (v.); compare
cognate Old Saxon slap, Old Frisian slep, Middle Dutch sl&aelig;p, Dutch slaap, Old High German
slaf, German Schlaf, Gothic sleps.  <br /><br />  Personified in English from late 14c., on model of
Latin <a href=/index.php?term=Somnus&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>Somnus</a>), Greek
Hypnos. Figurative use for repose of death was in Old English; to put (an animal) to sleep kill
painlessly is recorded from 1923 (a similar imagery is in <a
href=/index.php?term=cemetery&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>cemetery</a>). Sleep
deprivation attested from 1906. Sleep-walker somnambulist is attested from 1747; sleep-walking is
from 1840. To be able to do something in (one's) sleep easily is recorded from 1953.
