late 14c., capable of perceiving, fitted for mental impression, from Medieval Latin apprehensivus,
from Latin apprehensus, past participle of apprehendere (see <a
href=/index.php?term=apprehend&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>apprehend</a>). Meaning
fearful of what is to come is recorded from 1718, via notion of capable of grasping with the mind
(c.1600). Related: Apprehensively; apprehensiveness.
