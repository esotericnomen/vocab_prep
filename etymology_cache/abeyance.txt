1520s, from Anglo-French abeiance suspension, also expectation (especially in a lawsuit), from Old
French abeance aspiration, desire, noun of condition of abeer aspire after, gape from &agrave; at
(see <a href=/index.php?term=ad-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>ad-</a>) + ba(y)er be
open, from Latin *batare to yawn, gape (see <a href=/index.php?term=abash&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>abash</a>).  <br /><br />  Originally in French a legal term, condition of a
person in expectation or hope of receiving property; it turned around in English law to mean
condition of property temporarily without an owner (1650s). Root baer is also the source of English
<a href=/index.php?term=bay&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>bay</a> (n.2) recessed
space, as in bay window.
