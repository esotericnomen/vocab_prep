late 14c., from Anglo-French disporter divert, amuse, from Old French desporter to seek amusement,
literally carry away (the mind from serious matters), from des- away (see <a
href=/index.php?term=dis-&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>dis-</a>) + porter to carry,
from Latin portare to carry (see <a href=/index.php?term=port&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>port</a> (n.1)). Compare disporter a minstrel or jester (early 15c.).
