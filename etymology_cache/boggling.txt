mind-boggling (adj.)

1964; see mind (n.) + present participle of boggle.


boggle (v.)

1590s, to start with fright (as a startled horse does), shy, take alarm, from Middle English bugge
specter (among other things, supposed to scare horses at night); see bug (n.); also compare bogey
(n.1). The meaning to raise scruples, hesitate is from 1630s. As a noun from 1650s. Related:
Boggled; boggling; boggler (from c. 1600 as one who hesitates).
