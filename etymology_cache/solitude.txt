mid-14c., from Old French solitude loneliness (14c.) and directly from Latin solitudinem (nominative
solitudo) loneliness, a being alone; lonely place, desert, wilderness, from solus alone (see <a
href=/index.php?term=sole&amp;allowed_in_frame=0 class=crossreference>sole</a> (adj.)). Not in
common use in English until the 17th c. [OED]  <blockquote>A man can be himself only so long as he
is alone; ... if he does not love solitude, he will not love freedom; for it is only when he is
alone that he is really free. [Schopenhauer, The World as Will and Idea, 1818]</blockquote>
Solitudinarian recluse, unsocial person is recorded from 1690s.
