sluggard (n.)

late 14c., late 13c. as a surname, habitually lazy person, from Middle English sluggi sluggish,
indolent, probably from a Scandinavian word such as dialectal Norwegian slugga be sluggish,
dialectal Norwegian sluggje heavy, slow person, dialectal Swedish slogga to be slow or sluggish.
Adjective sluggy is attested in English from early 13c.  <blockquote>  'Tis the voice of a sluggard
-- I heard him complain:  You have wak'd me too soon, I must slumber again.  [Isaac Watts,
1674-1748]   <hr />   'Tis the voice of the Lobster: I heard him declare  You have baked me too
brown, I must sugar my hair.  [Lewis Carroll (Charles L. Dodgson), 1832-1898]   </blockquote>  As an
adjective meaning sluggish, lazy from 1590s. Related: Sluggardly.
