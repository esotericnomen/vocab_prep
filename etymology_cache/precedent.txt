early 15c., case which may be taken as a rule in similar cases, from Middle French precedent, noun
use of an adjective, from Latin praecedentum (nominative praecedens), present participle of
praecedere go before (see <a href=/index.php?term=precede&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>precede</a>). Meaning thing or person that goes before another is attested from
mid-15c. As an adjective in English from c.1400. As a verb meaning to furnish with a precedent from
1610s, now only in past participle <a href=/index.php?term=precedented&amp;allowed_in_frame=0
class=crossreference>precedented</a>.
